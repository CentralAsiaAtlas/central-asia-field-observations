# Mongolia Field Observations (2017)

## Introduction

In April 2017, I spent two weeks living and working with a nomadic herding family in Altanbulag District, Töv Province, Mongolia.

This was not a research project, nor did I have any intention of pursuing a career in international development. I simply wanted to experience daily life in a nomadic community rather than visit Mongolia as a tourist.

These notes are adapted from observations I recorded during that visit. They are presented chronologically because the sequence reflects how my understanding developed during my time in the camp.

The purpose of these field observations is not to describe Mongolia, but to document the moments that challenged my assumptions and eventually led me to ask broader questions about agriculture, rural livelihoods, culture, environmental systems, and, later, international development.

---

## Field Observation 1: Learning Through Observation

The first challenge I encountered was communication. My host family spoke little English, and I spoke almost no Mongolian. A small phrase book helped occasionally, but most conversations relied on gestures, demonstrations, and patience.

Rather than becoming frustrated, I found myself paying much closer attention to what people were doing than to what they were saying. Every task became an opportunity to learn by watching.

Within my first few hours at the camp, I witnessed something I had never considered before. One of the family's goats rejected its newborn kid. It was refusing to nurse the kid, and my first job in the camp was to hold the mother in place so the baby could feed. I had not thought that this type of maternal rejection could occur in animals, and I started drawing parallels between this observation and humans giving up their babies/newborns for adoption. Humans and animals were displaying the same behavior.

Helping the family work made me realize that successful herding also depended on understanding the behavior of individual animals. To me, all the goats and sheep just looked like goats and sheep, but the nomads, out of a herd of 50+ animals in this case, could individually identify each animal and its corresponding offspring. They even knew the "personalities" of some of the animals.

They had developed skills specific to their work, such as recognition and memory. These skills were just as important as physical labor.

I realized that there was some level of sophistication and knowledge in the nomads' work.

### Questions

* How common is maternal rejection in goats?
* How is practical knowledge about animal behavior passed from one generation to the next?
* What aspects of herding can only be learned through experience?

That experience changed the kinds of problems I became interested in understanding.

## Field Observation 2: The Disappearing Nomadic Way of Life

That first day, I learned that the family had two school-age children who lived with their grandparents in the nearby township so they could attend school. The parents did not expect, or even want, their children to become nomadic herders.

Earlier that day, during the drive from Ulaanbaatar to the camp, I had noticed several permanent ger settlements on the outskirts of the city. Before coming to Mongolia, I had read about the gradual transition from nomadic to sedentary life in Central Asia. Seeing both the permanent settlements and my host family's hopes for their children made that transition feel real. It was no longer something I had read about in a book—it was a decision being made by one family for the next generation.

It seemed the family genuinely enjoyed their lifestyle and took pride in their work and culture, but they also recognized that it was physically demanding and offered fewer opportunities than they wanted for their children.

I realized that traditional ways of life do not necessarily disappear because people stop valuing them. Sometimes they disappear because parents simply want an easier or more secure future for the next generation.

### Questions

* What problems is migration from the steppe to the city creating?
* What proportion of children eventually return to herding?
* How is Mongolia preserving nomadic traditions as fewer young people enter pastoral life?
* Did the nomads idolize or perceive city life as superior to their own?
